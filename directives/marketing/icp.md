## 2. Customer Avatar: "Alex"

**Name:** Alex
**Age:** 27
**Gender:** Male (though ~20% of AI founders are female — the avatar applies regardless of gender)
**Location:** San Francisco, New York, Austin, London, or remote-first — the AI startup ecosystem is geographically distributed but culturally concentrated on X and in YC/accelerator networks
**Occupation:** Co-Founder & CEO (or solo founder) of an AI tool
**Company Size:** 2–4 people. Alex + 1 co-founder (technical) + 1–2 engineers. Zero marketing, zero ops, zero content.
**Funding:** Pre-seed or Seed. Raised $1.2M–$4.6M (median pre-seed: $1.5M, median AI seed: $4.6M — Noizz.io / Pitchwise 2026). Alternatively, bootstrapped profitable at $10K–$50K MRR.
**Monthly Revenue:** $10K–$50K MRR. Real users. Real payments. Past the "idea stage" but before the "we have a marketing team" stage.
**Level of Education:** CS degree from a strong program. May have a Masters or PhD in ML/AI. Got into startups through building, not business school.
**Technical Background:** Deep. Alex can explain transformer architectures but struggles to explain what his tool does to a non-technical person in under 60 seconds.

### Goals

1. **Grow distribution without diverting engineering time.** Alex knows content is a compounding asset — he's watched competitors build audiences that feed organic acquisition — but every hour spent on content is an hour not spent shipping product. He needs distribution that doesn't require his daily attention.
2. **Build a personal brand as the founder.** He's seen founders like the Cursor team, Perplexity's Aravind Srinivas, and others gain massive distribution through founder-led content. He wants that, but doesn't know how to start or sustain it. Founder-led content generates 7x more impressions and 4x more engagement than company pages on LinkedIn (ContentBeta 2026).
3. **Convert awareness into pipeline.** The product is good — NPS is high, users love it — but growth has plateaued because acquisition is entirely word-of-mouth and Product Hunt launch traffic has faded. He needs a repeatable content-to-pipeline engine.
4. **Look legitimate to investors and customers.** A consistent, high-quality content presence signals traction, professionalism, and market authority. Investors check social presence before taking meetings. B2B buyers consume 3–5 pieces of content before reaching out (Contensify 2026).

### Values

1. **Speed and efficiency.** Alex ships fast. He expects vendors to move at startup speed, not agency speed. If something takes 3 weeks, he's already moved on.
2. **Authenticity over polish.** He doesn't want corporate brand content. He wants content that sounds like him — technically sharp, slightly irreverent, builder-to-builder. 68% of consumers prefer videos featuring real people (Animoto 2026). Alex instinctively knows this.
3. **Leverage through systems.** Alex thinks in terms of automation, compounding returns, and asymmetric bets. Content-as-infrastructure resonates more than content-as-marketing.
4. **Product quality.** He pours obsessive energy into the product. The idea that a great product markets itself is his default assumption — and also the belief that's currently holding him back.

### Problems (Validated)

1. **No time for content — building the product consumes everything.** AI startups at 1–5 people have zero slack. Every team member is engineering or product. Content falls to the founder by default, and the founder is already the CEO, head of product, and lead engineer. Marketing gets deprioritized weekly. The result: an inconsistent or nonexistent content presence that costs 12–18% higher customer acquisition costs versus competitors who post consistently (ContentBeta 2026).

2. **The distribution crisis is real and structural.** Traditional content distribution channels (SEO, blog posts, email) are collapsing. Search volume is projected to drop 25% by 2026 as users shift to AI answer engines (Resonancia Strategies 2026). Blog posts no longer guarantee rankings. The new distribution currency is short-form video and founder-led social — exactly what Alex doesn't have time to produce. "Distribution is the rarest resource in marketing" (AI for Content Marketing 2026).

3. **Content homogenization makes AI-generated content invisible.** When every AI startup uses ChatGPT to write their tweets and blog posts, everything sounds the same. Forrester predicts B2B companies will lose $10B+ due to ungoverned AI content use (Resonancia Strategies 2026). Only distinctly human, founder-voiced content cuts through. But Alex can't produce that content at scale without help.

4. **Inconsistency kills growth more than bad content does.** Highly consistent posters receive 5x more engagement than inconsistent posters (Buffer 2026). Accounts posting 5–7x weekly see 3–5x more reach than sporadic posters. Alex posts once every 2–3 weeks when guilt overwhelms him, gets mediocre results, and concludes "content doesn't work for us." The problem isn't the content — it's the cadence. He needs a system, not motivation.

5. **Cannot explain the product simply on camera.** Technical founders over-explain. They lead with architecture instead of outcomes. They say "we use a fine-tuned LLM pipeline with RAG" when the user cares about "it saves you 4 hours a week." Wraith bridges this gap — translating technical depth into content that resonates with non-technical buyers and the algorithm simultaneously.

6. **Hiring a marketer is premature, risky, and expensive.** The first marketing hire for a startup costs $80K–$120K+ in salary, takes 2–3 months to ramp, and has a high failure rate — a bad early marketing hire burns 3–6 months of runway (StartsClub 2026). A fractional CMO costs $5,000–$18,000/month (HookLead 2026). At $2,000/mo, Wraith delivers full execution at a fraction of the cost and with zero ramp time.

### What Keeps Alex Up at Night (Validated With Industry Data)

1. **"Our competitors are everywhere online and we're invisible."** He opens X and sees a competitor's demo video with 500K views. Their product is objectively worse than his. But they're growing faster because they showed up and he didn't. Short-form video generates 2.5x more engagement than any other format and drives 67% more website traffic when multi-platform (ContentBeta 2026). Alex knows this intellectually but can't act on it.

2. **"We have an incredible product but growth has plateaued."** After the Product Hunt launch spike, organic growth flattened. Paid ads are expensive and the CAC math doesn't work yet. Content is the only scalable, compounding acquisition channel left — and he's not using it.

3. **"I keep telling myself I'll post this week and then I don't."** Content guilt is a weekly occurrence. He drafts tweets, rewrites them four times, decides they're not good enough, and closes the tab. The pattern repeats. Consistent creators get 5x more engagement (Buffer 2026). Alex gets zero because he never ships the content.

4. **"Investors keep asking about our distribution strategy and I don't have a good answer."** VCs are increasingly focused on distribution moats, not just product quality. A founder with no content presence is a red flag for Series A conversations. 63% of B2B buyers use social video to research purchases (TokPortal 2026) — investors know this.

5. **"I can't afford a marketing person yet, but I also can't keep being the marketing person."** He's trapped between two bad options: hire too early and risk runway, or keep doing it himself and watch growth stall. He doesn't know the third option — a done-for-you agency at a price point that makes the decision easy — exists.

### Where Alex Gets His Information

- **X (Twitter):** Primary platform. Lives in AI Twitter. Follows other founders, VCs, AI researchers. Scrolls daily. Posts sporadically. This is where he sees competitors going viral and where he feels the most content guilt. AI/tech X is the cultural center of his professional world.
- **LinkedIn:** Secondary but growing. Uses it for investor-facing content and professional credibility. Founder-led content on LinkedIn generates 7x more impressions than company pages (ContentBeta 2026). Posts occasionally, usually cross-posting from X.
- **YouTube:** Watches long-form technical content — Lex Fridman, Fireship, Two Minute Papers, AI Explained. Knows YouTube is valuable for SEO and discovery — 50.9% of B2B decision-makers use YouTube for product research (ContentBeta 2026) — but hasn't started producing.
- **Podcasts:** My First Million, Lenny's Podcast, The AI Podcast (NVIDIA), Latent Space, Lex Fridman. Listens during commute or workouts. Consumes founder growth stories and AI industry analysis.
- **Product Hunt:** Launches here. Monitors competitor launches. Returns to browse weekly. Over 31,000 AI products launched on Product Hunt in 2025 alone (Hunted.space 2026).
- **Hacker News:** Reads daily. Comments occasionally. Trusts HN's community filter for technical content quality. Skeptical of anything that feels like marketing — which is precisely why founder-led, authentic content works on him.
- **Y Combinator / Accelerator Networks:** If YC or another accelerator alumnus, the Slack, Forum, and alumni network are primary trust circles. Peer recommendations carry enormous weight.
- **Discord / Slack communities:** AI-specific communities, indie hacker groups, founder peer groups. Word of mouth in these channels is high-trust and high-conversion.

### Common Objections / Limiting Beliefs (Validated)

1. **"Nobody can explain my product as well as I can."** Partially true — and also the reason he never gets content made. He assumes content requires his direct involvement on every video. Wraith's model requires only 2.5 hours/month from the founder. The rest is produced from screen recordings, voice clones, and deep product understanding built during onboarding.

2. **"AI-generated content feels inauthentic and my audience will notice."** 83% of consumers can spot AI-generated video (Animoto 2026), and 36% say it lowers brand trust. Alex is right to be skeptical of pure AI slop. But Wraith doesn't produce AI slop — it produces founder-led, human-first content that uses AI as a production accelerator, not a replacement. The founder's face, voice, and ideas are the content. AI handles editing, visuals, and scale.

3. **"I tried posting for a while and it didn't work."** He posted 8 times over 3 months with no strategy, no consistency, and no distribution plan. Consistent posters get 5x more engagement (Buffer 2026). B2B companies delaying short-form video face 12–18% higher CAC (ContentBeta 2026). The problem was never "content doesn't work" — it was "inconsistent content without strategy doesn't work."

4. **"Content agencies don't understand technical products."** He's right about most agencies. Generic content shops produce surface-level blog posts that technical buyers see through instantly. Wraith's entire business is founders building in AI — the onboarding process includes a full product deep-dive, screen recording walkthrough, and competitor research before a single piece of content is produced.

5. **"$2,000/month is a lot when we're burning cash."** Relative to what? A full-time marketer costs $80K–$120K+ annually plus 3 months to ramp. A fractional CMO costs $5K–$18K/month. Paid ads at the same spend level generate temporary traffic with no compounding effect. $2,000/month for 20 pieces of content that compound in value every month is the cheapest scalable distribution option available.

6. **"We should focus on product first and marketing later."** The #1 startup killer. In the AI content homogenization era, distribution IS the moat. "The product that wins is often not the best — it's the most visible" is a truism Alex has seen play out but hasn't internalized for his own company.

### Alex's Average Day (Informed by Founder Behavior Patterns)

Wakes up at 7:30 AM. Immediately checks X on his phone — sees a competitor's product demo clip with 200K views and 400 replies. Feels a pang of frustration. Opens Slack — three messages from his co-founder about a bug in production, two from users requesting features, one from an investor asking for a monthly update. Gets to his desk by 8:30, coffee in hand. Spends the morning in deep work — shipping code, reviewing PRs, debugging a critical issue in the inference pipeline. Eats lunch at his desk while scrolling X again. Sees another AI founder posting a "building in public" thread that's getting massive engagement. Opens a notes app, starts drafting a tweet about a feature they shipped yesterday. Rewrites it three times. Decides it sounds too self-promotional. Closes the app. Afternoon: a user interview at 2 PM (goes well — the user loves the product), a sync with his co-founder at 3:30 PM about the product roadmap, and then back to coding. At 5 PM he gets a calendar reminder: "Record content this week." Snoozes it. By 7 PM he's still at his desk, now responding to investor emails and updating the fundraising CRM. Opens his social accounts one more time before bed — his last post was 19 days ago. A competitor posted 6 times this week. He tells himself "I'll batch some content this weekend." He won't. Falls asleep thinking about a technical problem in the product, not content. The cycle repeats.

## 3. Market Sophistication: Level 2 (Transitioning to Level 3)

The market for **done-for-you content services specifically for AI tool companies** is at **Stage 2**, with early signs of moving to Stage 3.

**Evidence (sourced):**

- **No dominant category leader in the niche.** Generic content agencies exist by the thousands. Short-form video agencies exist. But nobody has built a recognizable brand around done-for-you founder-led content production exclusively for AI tool companies at the $1,200–$3,500/month price point. The niche is unoccupied.

- **Awareness is building but not saturated.** AI founders know they need content. They've heard of agencies. Some have tried Fiverr editors or freelance ghostwriters. But the concept of a vertically specialized, done-for-you video content operation for their specific market is new. They're not yet comparing five providers in this niche — because five providers don't exist yet.

- **Adjacent markets are at Stage 3–4.** Generic content marketing, social media management, and general video production agencies are deeply commoditized. Founders get cold-pitched by these agencies weekly. They've developed skepticism toward generic "we'll handle your content" pitches. But a pitch that says "we make founder-led short-form video for founders building in AI" still registers as novel.

- **The competition is structural, not direct.** Wraith's competition isn't another AI-content agency — it's the founder's own belief that they should do it themselves, that they'll get to it eventually, or that content doesn't matter yet. The "do nothing" competitor is the biggest threat.

**What Stage 2 means for our approach:** We can still lead with a direct claim — "done-for-you content for founders building in AI" — because the niche positioning itself is the differentiator. The market hasn't heard this specific pitch enough times to be numb to it. However, the adjacent skepticism from Stage 3+ generic agency bombardment means our messaging must immediately signal that we are NOT a generic shop. Specificity is our mechanism. The fact that we only serve founders building in AI IS the unique mechanism. As the market matures, we will need to layer in proof (case studies, ROI data, testimonials) to maintain differentiation — but for now, niche focus and production quality are the primary weapons.

## 4. Target Awareness Level: Level 2–3 — Problem Aware to Solution Aware

Alex **knows** he has a distribution problem. He feels it every time he sees a competitor's viral post. He **vaguely knows** solutions exist — agencies, freelancers, tools — but hasn't seriously evaluated any because none have been presented in a way that felt relevant to his specific situation.

**Breakdown:**

- **Problem aware?** Absolutely. He knows content matters. He knows he's not doing it. He feels the gap daily. The pain is acute but not yet channeled toward a specific solution — it manifests as guilt, frustration, and "I'll do it later."
- **Solution aware?** Partially. He knows content agencies exist. He's seen ads for social media management tools. He may have tried Canva, Buffer, or ChatGPT-generated posts. But he hasn't seen a service specifically designed for founders like him — AI-native, done-for-you, minimal time commitment, founder-led content at his price point.
- **Product aware (of us)?** No. He hasn't heard of Wraith.
- **Most aware?** Not possible yet — no marketing has reached him.

**What Level 2–3 awareness means for our approach:** Alex needs to be shown that his specific problem — "I'm an AI founder who knows content matters but can't find time to produce it and doesn't trust generic agencies to understand my product" — has a specific solution designed for exactly that situation. Cold outreach must name his exact pain, demonstrate deep understanding of his product before the first call, and make the offer feel inevitable rather than salesy. The message isn't "hire a content agency" — it's "you build the product, we build the audience, and it takes you 2.5 hours a month."

## 8. External Positioning vs. Internal Qualification

The ICP serves two purposes that require different levels of specificity. External positioning language is what prospects see — it must be sharp, self-selecting, and natural. Internal qualification criteria are what we use to build prospect lists and decide who to reach out to — these can be broader without diluting the brand.

### External Positioning (Copy-Facing)

Use these phrases in outreach, website copy, ads, and content. They're designed so the right person reads them and thinks "that's me."

| Context | Language |
|---------|----------|
| **Headlines / subject lines** | "AI founders" |
| **Longer copy / descriptions** | "founders building in AI" |
| **Pain framing** | "You don't have a content person" / "You're the CEO and the marketing department" |
| **Offer framing** | "You build the product, we build the audience" |
| **Price anchoring** | "20 videos for $100/video" or "$2,000/mo — less than a freelancer, more than an agency" |

Do NOT use in copy: funding stage labels, MRR ranges, team size numbers, or category lists ("AI tools, devtools, infra, APIs"). These filter internally, not externally. The prospect self-selects based on identity ("I'm a founder building in AI") and situation ("I don't have anyone doing content"), not based on reading their own metrics back to them.

### Internal Qualification (Prospect List Building)

Use these criteria when scraping Product Hunt, filtering Crunchbase, building outreach lists, or deciding whether a lead is worth pursuing. These are broader than the external positioning on purpose — the copy does the final filtering.

| Dimension | Criteria | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **Core product** | AI/ML is the core product, not a feature bolt-on | Includes AI tools, AI-native devtools, ML infrastructure, AI-powered APIs, data/ML platforms. Excludes companies that just "added AI" to an existing non-AI product. |
| **Funding stage** | Pre-seed through early Series A | Series A companies with no/minimal marketing team have the same pain and more budget. Don't exclude them. |
| **Revenue** | $10K+ MRR OR raised $1M+ (funded pre-revenue is valid) | For bootstrapped: MRR proves they can pay. For funded: the raise itself is the budget signal. |
| **Team size** | No dedicated content/video marketer | The qualifier is the absence of the role, not the headcount. A 15-person startup with 13 engineers and no marketer is a perfect fit. |
| **Geography (Phase 1)** | US, Canada, UK, Western Europe | Expand internationally after Month 3. |
| **Timing signals** | Post-accelerator, post-Product Hunt launch, post-funding announcement, growth plateau, competitor going viral | These are outreach triggers, not hard requirements. |

## 9. Edge Cases & Learnings

_Updated as outreach and conversations reveal new intelligence._

- **"Building in AI" means the AI IS the product, not a feature.** The core qualifier is whether the product would exist without the underlying AI/ML. This includes AI tools, AI-native developer tools, ML infrastructure, AI-powered APIs, and data/ML platforms. It excludes companies that bolted AI onto an existing non-AI product (e.g., a CRM that added a chatbot). Those companies are less technical, less embedded in the AI ecosystem, and less responsive to Wraith's positioning. In copy, "founders building in AI" is broad enough to capture the full range of valid targets without needing to list subcategories.

- **Bootstrapped vs funded creates different buying psychology.** Funded founders see $2,000/month as a line item against runway and evaluate it in terms of growth potential. Bootstrapped profitable founders see it as a cash outflow against revenue and evaluate it in terms of ROI. Messaging must flex: for funded → "invest in distribution before your runway dictates it." For bootstrapped → "20 videos for $100/video. The math works."

- **The "do nothing" competitor is the biggest threat.** Most lost deals won't be to another agency. They'll be to inaction — "I'll handle content myself eventually." Outreach must create urgency without manufactured scarcity. The most effective urgency trigger is showing Alex what competitors are doing RIGHT NOW, with specific examples, views, and growth metrics.

- **Solo founders vs co-founder teams have different blockers.** Solo founders have zero bandwidth and maximum guilt. They're the easiest to convert because the pain is most acute. Co-founder teams sometimes have one person who "was supposed to handle marketing" but hasn't — that creates internal tension that makes the sale more complex (two decision-makers).

- **International founders are viable but payment logistics matter.** AI startups are global. European, Indian, Southeast Asian, and Latin American founders are responsive to the pitch. But payment in USD, timezone alignment for check-in calls, and English-language content quality vary. Phase 1: US, Canada, UK, Western Europe. Expand further internationally after Month 3.

- **Post-accelerator is the sweetest timing.** Founders who just finished YC, Techstars, or another accelerator have: fresh funding, refined pitch, pressure from investors to grow, and a network that validates agency hires. YC Demo Day timing (March and September) creates a predictable wave of ideal prospects.

- **Product Hunt launch timing creates a natural trigger.** A founder who just launched on Product Hunt is in peak visibility-seeking mode. They just experienced the high of traffic and attention — and the crash when it fades. Outreach within 1–2 weeks of a Product Hunt launch has the highest conversion potential.

- **Series A founders are valid targets when the content gap exists.** Early Series A companies (raised $8–15M, 10–20 people) frequently still have zero or one junior marketer who's focused on blog posts or product marketing — not founder-led video content. They have more budget, similar urgency, and often a board explicitly pushing "fix distribution." Don't disqualify based on funding stage alone; qualify based on whether the content gap exists.

- **Validation needed:** 5–10 actual discovery calls with AI founders in the target profile will sharpen the avatar dramatically. The research covers the "what" well but real conversations will reveal the emotional texture, specific language, and hidden objections that data can't capture. First 30 manual outreach messages should be treated as research, not just pipeline generation.
